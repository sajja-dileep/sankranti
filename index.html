Sankranti, also known as Makar Sankranti, is a major Hindu festival celebrated in India and Nepal, marking the transition of the sun into the zodiac sign of Capricorn (Makar). It symbolizes new beginnings, prosperity, and the end of the chilly winter, welcoming the arrival of warmer days. The first day begins

Bonfires

A central ritual of Bhogi involves the lighting of a bonfire. People collect old and unwanted items such as clothes, mats, furniture, and agricultural waste, which are then ceremoniously burned in a bonfire. This act symbolizes the act of letting go of the past and embracing a fresh start, ushering in the spring season. It is believed that this ritual helps dispel negative energies and invites prosperity into people's lives.

Social gatherings

Bhogi is a time for families and friends to come together, exchange gifts, and share joyous moments. It strengthens social bonds and fosters a sense of unity and harmony.Overall, social gatherings during Bhogi play a crucial role in fostering a sense of unity, camaraderie, and shared joy among family members, friends, and neighbors. It's a time when people come together, leaving aside differences, to celebrate the essence of the festival.

Bhogi

Bhogi, which is the first day of the Makar Sankranti festival celebrated in South India, is marked by several rituals and customs. One such tradition associated with Bhogi is the preparation of "Bhogi Pallu" or "Bhogi Manta" in some regions, especially in Andhra Pradesh and Telangana.Bhogi Pallu is a custom where special items, including Bhogi rice, are used as offerings to mark the day. Bhogi rice is specifically selected for its significance in this ritual.

Day two begins

Rangoli

During Sankranti, households create rangoli designs outside their homes to invite prosperity and good luck. Rangoli is crafted by forming patterns on the floor using colored rice, dry flour, sand, flower petals, or other natural materials. These designs, often geometric or floral, adorn the entrance of the house. The process involves careful and precise movements to create symmetrical and aesthetically pleasing patterns. The colors used in rangoli carry significance; red signifies strength, blue represents calmness, yellow symbolizes positivity, and green embodies harmony. Families usually gather together to partake in this joyful and communal activity of designing rangoli.

Flying kites

Flying kites is a cherished tradition during the Sankranti festival, particularly in several states of India like Gujarat, Rajasthan, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Andhra Pradesh. It's an integral part of the celebrations and is enjoyed by people of all ages. During Sankranti, skies become adorned with colorful kites of various shapes and sizes. The activity of kite flying is not just a leisurely pastime.

last day of sankranthi

Kanuma is a festival celebrated as part of the Makar Sankranti festivities in some parts of South India, particularly in the state of Andhra Pradesh. Makar Sankranti is a harvest festival celebrated across India in mid-January, marking the transition of the sun into the zodiac sign of Capricorn (Makara).On Kanuma, cattle, especially cows and bullocks, are bathed, adorned with garlands, vermilion, and turmeric, and offered special treats as a mark of respect and thanksgiving for their role in farming activities.Overall, Kanuma during Makar Sankranti is a celebration that emphasizes the significance of cattle and their contribution to the livelihoods of farmers, alongside the broader theme of harvest and thanksgiving.

Cattle Worship

Cattle worship during Sankranti, particularly on the day of Kanuma in some regions, is a significant cultural practice in South India, especially in states like Andhra Pradesh and Telangana.
